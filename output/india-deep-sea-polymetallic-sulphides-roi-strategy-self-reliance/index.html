India’s Deep-Sea Bet: Turning Polymetallic Sulphides into Strategic Metal Security — Timeline, ROI & Roadmap

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-02  | ⏱ 7 min read

India’s recent 15-year exploration contract with the International Seabed Authority (ISA)

For polymetallic sulphides (PMS) on the Carlsberg Ridge in the Indian Ocean marks a strategic step toward securing critical metals such as copper, cobalt, gold, and zinc




As global demand for battery metals and industrial minerals rises due to electrification, electric vehicles, and renewable energy infrastructure, countries are seeking diversified supply chains. For India, deep-sea mining could eventually become a strategic pillar of its critical minerals policy.

India’s move into deep-sea polymetallic sulphide exploration also reflects a broader shift in global resource geopolitics, where access to critical minerals is increasingly linked to national security and industrial competitiveness. As traditional land-based mining faces environmental constraints, declining ore grades, and geopolitical concentration risks, the deep ocean is emerging as a frontier that could reshape long-term supply dynamics. For India, early participation allows not just resource access but also influence in shaping global seabed mining norms.

Why Deep-Sea Minerals Matter for India

The global energy transition is dramatically increasing demand for metals used in electric vehicles, power grids, electronics, and renewable technologies.

Key metals found in seafloor polymetallic sulphide deposits include:

  • Copper: essential for power grids, EV motors, and renewable energy systems

  • Cobalt: used in battery cathodes and high-temperature alloys

  • Gold and silver: vital for electronics and strategic reserves

  • Zinc and molybdenum: used in steel, industrial alloys, and manufacturing

Securing access to these metals will strengthen India’s industrial resilience and supply chain security.

In addition, the electrification of transport and expansion of renewable energy systems are expected to multiply India’s demand for copper and battery-related metals over the next two decades. Domestic production alone is unlikely to meet this surge, making diversified sourcing essential. Deep-sea resources, while complex, offer a potential hedge against supply shocks, price volatility, and overdependence on a limited number of exporting countries.

India’s Deep-Sea Mining Roadmap

Exploration and Environmental Baseline (2025–2028)

India will first conduct high-resolution seabed mapping, geological sampling, and ecological studies in the Carlsberg Ridge area.

Lead institutions:

  • Ministry of Earth Sciences (MoES)

  • National Centre for Polar and Ocean Research (NCPOR)

This phase will determine:

  • mineral resource size

  • metal grades

  • ecological sensitivity of deep-sea habitats

This phase will also play a crucial role in building India’s domestic scientific and technological capabilities in oceanography, marine geology, and deep-sea robotics. High-quality baseline data will not only guide resource estimation but also serve as a reference point for future environmental impact assessments, ensuring that any deviation caused by human activity can be accurately measured and managed.

Pilot Extraction and Metallurgical Testing (2028–2032)

Once exploration data confirms viable deposits, India will likely move to limited pilot extraction operations.

Objectives include:

  • testing robotic deep-sea collection systems

  • validating ore lifting technologies

  • developing hydrometallurgical processing methods onshore

India may partner with:

  • domestic shipyards

  • public sector enterprises

  • International deep-sea technology providers

Equally important will be the optimization of ore processing techniques, as polymetallic sulphides require complex separation processes to economically extract multiple metals. India’s ability to develop efficient and cost-effective hydrometallurgical methods will directly influence project viability, making R&D investment and international collaboration critical during this phase.
Regulatory and Environmental Governance(2025 Ongoing)

Commercial seabed mining cannot begin until final exploitation regulations from the International Seabed Authority are established.

India will also need:

  • national environmental clearance frameworks

  • continuous ocean monitoring systems

  • independent scientific oversight

Environmental risks include:

  • sediment plumes

  • disturbance of hydrothermal vent ecosystems

  • biodiversity loss in deep-sea habitats

Strong governance will be essential to ensure responsible development.

India may also need to establish an independent regulatory body or strengthen existing institutions to oversee compliance, data transparency, and environmental monitoring. Lessons from terrestrial mining suggest that early integration of ESG frameworks not only reduces long-term risks but also improves investor confidence and international credibility in emerging sectors like deep-sea mining.

Building the Critical Minerals Value Chain (2028–2035)

The real economic value of deep-sea minerals lies not only in extraction but also in downstream processing and manufacturing.

India must invest in:

  • coastal refining facilities

  • battery-grade metal processing

  • copper and cobalt smelting

  • recycling and urban mining infrastructure

Currently, China dominates global refining of critical minerals, making downstream investment essential for India’s strategic autonomy.

Developing this value chain domestically will have multiplier effects across India’s manufacturing ecosystem, including job creation, technology transfer, and export competitiveness. By linking deep-sea resource extraction with initiatives such as electric vehicle manufacturing and renewable energy deployment, India can ensure that raw material access translates into tangible economic growth.

Conditional Commercial Scale-Up (2030s onward)

Large-scale mining would only occur if:

  • pilot extraction proves technically viable

  • environmental safeguards are validated

  • International regulations allow exploitation

Production would likely scale gradually through phased commercial projects.

A phased approach to scaling will also allow policymakers to incorporate technological improvements and evolving environmental standards over time. This adaptive strategy reduces the risk of large upfront commitments while enabling continuous learning from pilot operations and global best practices in deep-sea mining.

Financial Outlook: ROI Potential and Risks

Deep-sea polymetallic sulphide mining is high-risk and capital-intensive.

Estimated investment pathway:

  • Pilot development: $200–500 million

  • Commercial deployment: $1–2 billion

Project profitability depends heavily on:

  • copper and cobalt prices

  • ore grades

  • recovery efficiency

  • environmental compliance costs

In favorable scenarios, projects could achieve positive net present value (NPV) over 15–25 years, but cost overruns or low metal grades could significantly reduce returns.

Investors and policymakers will need to adopt a scenario-based approach, factoring in fluctuations in global metal prices, technological advancements, and regulatory developments. Sensitivity analysis across different cost and price assumptions will be essential to avoid overestimation of returns and ensure prudent capital allocation in such high-risk, long-gestation projects.

India’s Strategic Self-Sufficiency Outlook

Short Term (0–5 years)

Focus remains on exploration and scientific research.

Medium Term (5–15 years)

India could begin supplying a portion of its own critical metals, especially if refining capacity expands.

Long Term (15+ years)

Deep-sea resources could become part of a diversified national critical minerals strategy, alongside:

  • overseas mining partnerships

  • domestic recycling

  • strategic stockpiling

At the same time, deep-sea mining should be seen as complementary to other strategies such as securing overseas mining assets, strengthening trade partnerships, and investing in circular economy practices. A diversified approach will provide greater resilience than relying on any single source of critical minerals.

Key Risks to Watch

Environmental Risks

Deep-sea ecosystems are among the least understood on Earth. Mining could cause long-term ecological disruption.

Regulatory Uncertainty

International seabed mining rules are still evolving, which could affect project timelines.

Technical and Financial Challenges

Deep-sea mining technology is still emerging, and capital costs remain extremely high.

Public perception and international environmental advocacy could also influence the pace of development, as deep-sea mining remains a contentious issue globally. Proactive stakeholder engagement, transparency in data sharing, and adherence to global best practices will be essential to maintaining legitimacy and avoiding reputational risks.

Strategic Takeaway

India’s Carlsberg Ridge exploration contract should be viewed as a long-term strategic option rather than an immediate revenue source.

By investing in scientific research, pilot mining technology, downstream refining, and recycling infrastructure, India can gradually build a resilient supply chain for critical metals needed in the energy transition.

If executed carefully with strong environmental safeguards, deep-sea polymetallic sulphides could become a future source of strategic minerals for India’s industrial growth.

Ultimately, success in this domain will depend on India’s ability to align scientific capability, industrial policy, and environmental stewardship into a coherent long-term strategy. Rather than pursuing rapid commercialization, a calibrated and knowledge-driven approach will position India as both a responsible and competitive player in the emerging deep-sea economy.
