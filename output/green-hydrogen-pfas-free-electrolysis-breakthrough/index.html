Scientists May Have Found a Way to Fix Green Hydrogen’s Biggest Problem With PFAS-Free Electrolysis

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-10  | ⏱ 5 min read

Scientists May Have Found a Way to Fix Green Hydrogen’s Biggest Problem

Green hydrogen is widely regarded as one of the most promising solutions for achieving a global clean energy transition. 

It offers a carbon-free alternative for industries that are difficult to electrify and provides a reliable way to store excess renewable energy from wind and solar power.

However, despite its immense potential, green hydrogen still faces two major challenges: high production costs and environmental concerns related to the materials used in electrolysis systems.

A new European research initiative called SUPREME is attempting to solve these problems by developing PFAS-free electrolysis technology that is both efficient and economically viable.

Green hydrogen is not just an alternative fuel—it represents a complete transformation in how energy is produced, stored, and utilized. As nations aim for net-zero emissions, hydrogen is emerging as a strategic energy carrier bridging gaps where electrification alone falls short. Its scalability and compatibility with existing industrial infrastructure make it indispensable. Yet, unless cost and sustainability challenges are addressed, its adoption could slow down—making innovations like PFAS-free electrolysis absolutely critical.

One of the most widely used technologies for producing green hydrogen is Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis, known for its efficiency with fluctuating renewable energy inputs.

However, current PEM systems depend heavily on:

  • PFAS (“forever chemicals”)
  • Iridium, a rare and expensive metal

These dependencies significantly increase both cost and environmental impact.

This creates a paradox: a technology designed to reduce environmental harm introduces new ecological risks. PFAS chemicals persist in nature and are linked to long-term health hazards, while iridium scarcity leads to supply chain instability and price volatility. These challenges not only inflate costs but also weaken investor confidence and policy momentum. Solving these bottlenecks is essential for unlocking hydrogen’s full potential.

The SUPREME Project: Reinventing Hydrogen Electrolysis

The SUPREME project, led by the University of Southern Denmark along with partners like TU Graz, is a breakthrough initiative aimed at transforming hydrogen production.

Its core goals include:

  • Developing PFAS-free materials
  • Reducing iridium usage by 75%
  • Achieving up to 90% recyclability

This project represents a powerful collaboration between academia, industry, and policymakers. Instead of incremental improvements, it focuses on systemic innovation—combining material science, engineering, and sustainability principles. By aligning with circular economy goals, SUPREME ensures that resources are reused efficiently. If successful, it could redefine global standards for clean hydrogen production.

Testing PFAS-Free Materials for Industrial Use

Researchers at TU Graz are actively testing alternative PFAS-free materials that are already commercially available.

At the same time, TÜBITAK is developing advanced microporous membranes for next-generation electrolysis systems.

Material innovation is central to improving hydrogen technology. Performance, durability, and cost efficiency all depend on the materials used. By focusing on existing materials, researchers are accelerating the path to commercialization. These materials must withstand extreme operational conditions while maintaining efficiency. Meanwhile, microporous membranes offer the potential to reduce energy losses and boost performance, making hydrogen production more competitive.

Cutting Iridium Dependence

Reducing reliance on iridium is one of the most critical breakthroughs being pursued.

Key targets include:

  • 75% reduction in iridium usage
  • Up to 90% recycling efficiency

Iridium scarcity poses a major risk as hydrogen demand grows globally. By developing alternative catalysts and improving recycling systems, researchers are tackling both economic and environmental challenges. This also reduces geopolitical risks tied to rare material supply chains. In the long run, minimizing dependence on scarce resources will make green hydrogen more scalable and globally accessible.

New Electrolyser Designs

Innovation is also happening at the hardware level:

  • Fraunhofer ISE is developing advanced membrane units
  • Element One Energy is designing rotating electrolysers

These innovations aim to improve efficiency, reduce costs, and enhance system performance.

Engineering advancements are just as vital as material breakthroughs. New electrolyser designs can significantly lower energy consumption and operational costs. Rotating electrolysers introduce dynamic optimization, improving reaction efficiency and hydrogen output. Such innovations could dramatically reduce the cost per kilogram of hydrogen, making it commercially viable at scale.

Why Green Hydrogen Matters for the Future

Hydrogen already plays a role in industries such as:

  • Ammonia production
  • Methanol manufacturing
  • Steel production

It also serves as a powerful energy storage solution for surplus renewable power.

Green hydrogen has the potential to reshape global energy systems. It enables decarbonization in sectors where electrification is difficult, such as aviation and shipping. Additionally, it stabilizes power grids by storing excess renewable energy. As nations invest in hydrogen infrastructure, it is becoming a cornerstone of energy security and sustainability. Its widespread adoption could significantly accelerate the transition to a low-carbon economy.

A Step Toward a Sustainable Hydrogen Economy

The SUPREME project, funded under the EU’s Clean Energy Transition Partnership, aims to make hydrogen:

  • Cleaner
  • Cheaper
  • Scalable

Its success could accelerate the global shift toward sustainable energy systems.

This initiative represents more than just technological progress—it signals a turning point in the global energy transition. Addressing both cost and environmental concerns paves the way for large-scale hydrogen adoption. Governments, industries, and investors are closely watching such developments. If these technologies reach commercial viability, they could unlock massive economic opportunities while driving sustainability. Ultimately, this brings the world closer to a resilient and carbon-neutral future.
