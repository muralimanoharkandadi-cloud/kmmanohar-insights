India’s Rare-Earth-Free EV Motor Mission: How India Plans to Break China’s 90% Rare Earth Monopoly

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-13  | ⏱ 7 min read

 India’s Push for Rare-Earth-Free EV Motors: 

A Strategic Roadmap Toward Self-Reliant Electric Mobility

India’s electric vehicle (EV) journey is entering a decisive phase. As global supply chains face geopolitical disruptions and resource concentration risks, India has begun aggressively pursuing rare-earth-free electric motor technologies.

The Chintan Shivir on Alternate & Emerging Technologies in E-Motors, hosted by the Ministry of Heavy Industries at Vigyan Bhawan, New Delhi, marks one of the most significant policy-industry collaborations in India’s EV ecosystem.

This strategic dialogue was not merely an academic discussion. It was a national mission to reduce India’s heavy dependence on rare-earth magnets—90% of which are controlled by China—and to build a resilient domestic EV powertrain industry.

The urgency became clearer after China’s export curbs on rare-earth materials in April 2025, which disrupted EV supply chains worldwide. For India, which aims to become a global EV manufacturing hub, the message was unmistakable: self-reliance in motor technology is now a strategic necessity.

Most modern EV motors rely on Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motors (PMSMs) that use rare-earth elements such as:

  • Neodymium

  • Dysprosium

  • Praseodymium

These materials allow motors to achieve:

  • High torque density

  • Compact size

  • Higher efficiency

  • Lower energy loss

However, the problem lies in supply concentration.

China dominates:

  • ~90% of global rare-earth processing

  • A significant portion of magnet manufacturing

This concentration creates geopolitical and economic risks for EV-producing nations.

For a country like India that plans to electrify millions of vehicles across segments—from scooters to heavy trucks—depending on imported magnet technology is a strategic vulnerability.

Thus, India’s focus has shifted toward magnet-free and low-rare-earth motor technologies.

Held on December 5 at Vigyan Bhawan, the Chintan Shivir convened an influential group of stakeholders, including:

  • Government policymakers

  • Automotive manufacturers

  • EV startups

  • Research institutions

  • Component suppliers

  • Technology innovators

The objective was clear:

Design a technology roadmap that reduces dependence on rare-earth magnets while ensuring performance, affordability, and scalability.

Shri Kamran Rizvi, Secretary of the Ministry of Heavy Industries, emphasized that India must not merely react to supply disruptions but lead the global transition toward efficient and cost-effective EV motor technologies.

The discussions resulted in a segment-wise roadmap covering:

  • Two-wheelers

  • Three-wheelers

  • Passenger cars

  • Buses

  • Commercial vehicles

Each segment has different performance requirements, cost constraints, and technology maturity levels.

Segment-Wise EV Motor Technology Roadmap

The Chintan Shivir produced a structured roadmap for different vehicle categories, ensuring that the chosen motor technologies align with performance and affordability.

Two-Wheelers

India’s EV revolution is being led by electric scooters and motorcycles.

Because of their relatively low power requirements, they are ideal for adopting rare-earth-free motor technologies quickly.

Promising technologies include:

  • Switched Reluctance Motors (SRM)

  • Induction Motors

  • Ferrite Magnet Motors

Industry experts expect complete adoption of rare-earth-free motors in this segment by FY27.

Three-Wheelers

Electric auto-rickshaws represent one of India’s fastest-growing EV segments.

For this category, the preferred technologies include:

  • Wound Field Synchronous Motors

  • Switched Reluctance Motors

These options offer a balance of durability, torque, and cost efficiency, making them suitable for high-utilization urban transport vehicles.

Passenger Cars

Passenger vehicles require higher performance and efficiency.

Thus, the roadmap suggests a hybrid strategy:

  • Permanent-Magnet-Assisted Synchronous Motors

  • Reduced rare-earth designs

  • Advanced induction motors

These solutions aim to maintain driving performance while gradually reducing dependence on imported magnets.

Buses and Commercial Vehicles

Heavy vehicles demand:

  • High torque

  • Robust thermal management

  • Long operational life

Promising technologies include:

  • High-efficiency induction motors

  • Advanced switched reluctance motors

These designs eliminate magnets entirely while maintaining durability for fleet operations and logistics transport.

Several advanced motor technologies are now gaining momentum as alternatives to rare-earth magnet motors.

1. Induction Motors

Induction motors are one of the oldest and most reliable electric motor technologies.

Advantages include:

  • No rare-earth magnets

  • Lower material cost

  • Mature manufacturing ecosystem

However, they can be slightly heavier and less efficient at low speeds compared to PMSMs.

2. Switched Reluctance Motors (SRM)

SRMs are emerging as one of the most promising magnet-free technologies.

Key advantages:

  • Extremely robust design

  • No rare-earth materials

  • High torque capability

  • Simple rotor construction

The challenge lies in noise and vibration control, which modern control electronics are gradually solving.

3. Wound Field Synchronous Motors

These motors replace permanent magnets with electromagnetic windings on the rotor.

Benefits include:

  • Adjustable magnetic field strength

  • No dependence on rare-earth materials

  • Improved efficiency at varying speeds

This technology is already gaining traction in global EV development.

4. Ferrite Magnet Motors

Ferrite magnets are far cheaper and more abundant than rare-earth magnets.

Though less powerful, improvements in motor design and magnetic optimization are making ferrite-based motors increasingly viable for lightweight EV segments.

Industry Momentum: From Research to Production

India’s EV industry has already begun translating these ideas into real manufacturing initiatives.

A few notable developments include:

Simple Energy

The Bengaluru-based EV startup launched India’s first rare-earth-free electric motor in September 2025 at its Hosur manufacturing facility.

This breakthrough demonstrates that commercial magnet-free EV motors are already feasible.

Sterling Gtake

Sterling Tools’ subsidiary, Sterling Gtake E-Mobility, has been actively testing reluctance motor technology with seven automobile manufacturers.

Mass production is expected to begin by 2026.

Industry Adoption Timeline

Many EV manufacturers are targeting:

Full rare-earth-free motor adoption in two-wheelers and three-wheelers by FY2027.

This transition could significantly reduce India’s dependence on imported rare-earth materials.

India’s EV Powertrain Market: Rapid Growth Ahead

India’s EV powertrain sector is expanding at an extraordinary pace.

Industry forecasts suggest:

  • 28.5% CAGR growth

  • Market size reaching $9.31 billion by 2030

Key drivers include:

  • Rising EV adoption

  • Government incentives

  • Local manufacturing policies

  • Growing startup innovation

The powertrain—including motors, controllers, and transmission systems—represents one of the highest value components of an EV.

Thus, domestic capability in this area is strategically important.

Government Support Under Atmanirbhar Bharat

India’s EV self-reliance strategy aligns strongly with the Atmanirbhar Bharat (Self-Reliant India) initiative.

Government support focuses on:

R&D Investments

Funding is being directed toward:

  • Magnet-free motor designs

  • Advanced motor control algorithms

  • High-efficiency power electronics

Testing Infrastructure

India is strengthening EV testing facilities to evaluate:

  • Motor performance

  • Durability

  • Efficiency standards

This helps domestic manufacturers accelerate product development.

Indigenous Component Manufacturing

Policies aim to encourage:

  • Local manufacturing of stators and rotors

  • Domestic production of controllers and inverters

  • Local supply chains for motor materials

The ultimate goal is to build a complete EV powertrain ecosystem within India.

Strategic Implications for India

The push for rare-earth-free motors is about much more than EV technology.

It has national strategic significance.

Supply Chain Security

Reducing dependence on Chinese rare-earth materials strengthens India’s industrial autonomy.

Cost Competitiveness

Magnet-free motors can significantly reduce EV production costs.

Lower costs translate into:

  • More affordable EVs

  • Faster mass adoption

Global Technology Leadership

If India succeeds in scaling magnet-free EV motor technologies, it could become a global exporter of next-generation EV powertrains.

This would strengthen India’s role in the global clean mobility ecosystem.

Challenges Ahead

Despite the optimism, several challenges remain.

Performance Optimization

Magnet-free motors must match or exceed the efficiency and power density of PMSMs.

Manufacturing Ecosystem

Scaling new motor technologies requires:

  • New supply chains

  • Advanced manufacturing capabilities

  • Skilled engineering workforce

Industry Transition

Automakers must redesign vehicle platforms to accommodate different motor architectures.

This requires time and investment.

The Road Ahead: India’s EV Independence Moment

India’s push for rare-earth-free electric motors represents a bold step toward technological sovereignty in the EV sector.

By combining:

  • Policy leadership

  • Industry collaboration

  • Startup innovation

  • Indigenous R&D

India is building the foundations of a self-reliant EV powertrain ecosystem.

If the roadmap outlined at the Chintan Shivir succeeds, India could transition from being a technology importer to a global innovator in electric mobility.

For a country aiming to achieve net-zero emissions and large-scale EV adoption, this shift could prove transformative.

The coming decade may well determine whether India simply participates in the EV revolution—or leads it.
