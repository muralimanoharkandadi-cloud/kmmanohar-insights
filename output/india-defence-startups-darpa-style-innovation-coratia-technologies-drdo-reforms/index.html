India’s Defence Startups Dive Deep: From Underwater Robotics to a DARPA-Style Innovation Revolution

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-01  | ⏱ 7 min read

India’s Defence Startup Revolution: Coratia Technologies, DRDO Reforms, and....

The Rise of a DARPA-Style Innovation Ecosystem


India’s defence innovation ecosystem is undergoing a historic transformation. For decades, the country’s defence sector was dominated by large public sector undertakings and government laboratories. While these institutions built important capabilities, the pace of innovation was often constrained by bureaucratic processes, limited private participation, and rigid funding mechanisms.
Today, however, a new wave of defence startups is emerging, bringing agility, deep-tech expertise, and disruptive thinking into India’s national security architecture. 
One of the most striking examples of this shift is the recent success of Coratia Technologies, an underwater robotics startup that secured both investment and a significant defence contract within days.

This development coincides with a broader reform initiative announced by the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) aimed at creating a DARPA-style innovation ecosystem in India—potentially redefining how the nation funds and develops cutting-edge military technologies.

A Breakthrough Moment for Defence Startups
A key milestone in this transformation came when Coratia Technologies raised ₹5 crore from Piper Serica Angel Fund shortly after winning a ₹66 crore contract with the Indian Navy.
This is more than just another startup funding story. It represents a fundamental shift in the way India’s defence establishment engages with the private technology ecosystem.

Traditionally, defence procurement involved lengthy tender processes and large industrial contractors. Startups rarely had the financial capacity or track record to compete in this environment. But the success of Coratia demonstrates that the landscape is changing rapidly.
For the first time, young technology firms are not only building advanced defence systems but also attracting investor confidence and securing real operational contracts from the armed forces.

Why Underwater Robotics Matters for Modern Warfare

Underwater robotics is emerging as one of the most strategically important domains in modern naval warfare.

The world’s oceans carry enormous strategic significance—from submarine warfare and maritime surveillance to seabed infrastructure protection. Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) are now used for a variety of military missions, including:

  • Mine detection and neutralization

  • Submarine tracking and surveillance

  • Inspection of underwater infrastructure, such as pipelines and communication cables

  • Coastal security monitoring

  • Intelligence gathering in contested maritime zones

For a country like India—with a coastline of over 7,500 kilometers and vast maritime interests in the Indian Ocean Region (IOR)—such technologies are critical for maintaining naval superiority.

Startups like Coratia Technologies can accelerate the development of cost-effective, indigenous solutions tailored specifically for India’s operational requirements.

The Strategic Importance of Startup Participation in Defence

The involvement of startups in defence innovation is not just about new companies entering the sector. It represents a structural shift in how nations develop military technology.

Globally, cutting-edge defence innovations increasingly originate from private startups rather than traditional defence contractors. Technologies such as:

  • Artificial intelligence

  • Autonomous systems

  • Cybersecurity tools

  • Space technologies

  • Advanced sensors

  • Robotics

are often pioneered by small, agile companies capable of experimenting rapidly.

Countries that successfully integrate startups into their defence ecosystem gain a significant technological advantage.

India’s policymakers are increasingly recognizing this reality.

DRDO’s Plan for a DARPA-Style Innovation Model

During the Delhi Defence Dialogue 2025, DRDO Chief Dr. Samir V. Kamat announced an important reform aimed at accelerating defence innovation.

The proposal involves introducing a new chapter in India’s General Financial Rules (GFR) specifically designed to support research and development funding for:

  • Startups

  • MSMEs

  • Private industries

  • Technology innovators

This reform could transform how defence R&D is funded in India.

Currently, government financial regulations are designed primarily to ensure strict accountability for expenditure. While this approach is essential for transparency, it can slow down experimental research—especially when projects involve technological uncertainty.

Dr. Kamat emphasized that the new framework will help create an environment where high-risk innovation becomes possible.

Learning from the DARPA Model

The reform is inspired by the success of the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA).

DARPA is widely regarded as one of the most influential innovation agencies in history. Many revolutionary technologies originated from DARPA-funded research, including:

  • The internet (ARPANET)

  • GPS technology

  • Stealth aircraft technologies

  • Advanced robotics

  • Autonomous vehicles

What makes DARPA unique is its willingness to fund high-risk, high-reward research.

Unlike traditional government programs, DARPA accepts that many projects may fail. However, even failed experiments often generate knowledge that leads to breakthroughs elsewhere.

Dr. Kamat highlighted a key difference between the U.S. system and India’s current framework.

In the U.S., DARPA projects do not face intense scrutiny for failed outcomes because innovation is treated as a strategic investment rather than a guaranteed return expenditure.

India is now attempting to adopt a similar mindset.

The Cultural Shift: Accepting Failure in Innovation

One of the most significant insights shared by Dr. Kamat relates to the issue of failure in research and development.

Innovation rarely follows a straight path. Breakthrough technologies often emerge only after multiple unsuccessful experiments.

In India’s traditional public finance system, however, failed projects can trigger audits, inquiries, and reputational risks for decision-makers. This creates a culture of caution where administrators prefer safe, incremental projects over bold experimentation.

The proposed GFR reform aims to address this challenge by reframing defence R&D spending as strategic investment.

The logic is simple: even if a project fails, the knowledge gained can contribute to future technologies.

For example, failed prototypes may generate valuable insights into materials science, artificial intelligence algorithms, propulsion systems, or sensor technologies.

These lessons can later be applied across multiple defence programs.

The Rise of India’s Defence Startup Ecosystem

India already has the foundations for a thriving defence startup ecosystem.

Several government initiatives have been introduced in recent years to encourage private innovation in the defence sector, including:

Innovations for Defence Excellence (iDEX)

The iDEX program connects startups with the armed forces and defence organizations to solve real operational problems.

Startups receive funding support and opportunities to develop prototypes for military applications.

Defence Industrial Corridors

India has established two defence corridors in:

  • Uttar Pradesh

  • Tamil Nadu

These corridors aim to create clusters of defence manufacturing and technology companies.

Make in India in Defence

The government’s Atmanirbhar Bharat initiative encourages domestic production of defence equipment, reducing dependence on imports.

These initiatives are gradually creating a pipeline of startups capable of building sophisticated defence technologies.

Strategic Implications for India’s National Security

The growth of defence startups could significantly enhance India’s strategic capabilities.

First, indigenous innovation reduces reliance on foreign suppliers for critical military technologies. This is particularly important in times of geopolitical tension when supply chains may become uncertain.

Second, startup-driven innovation accelerates the pace of technological development. Small teams can experiment rapidly, iterate designs quickly, and adopt emerging technologies faster than traditional organizations.

Third, a vibrant defence startup ecosystem strengthens the country’s broader technological base.

Many military technologies eventually find civilian applications. Historically, defence innovations have led to advances in:

  • Telecommunications

  • Navigation systems

  • Aerospace engineering

  • Advanced materials

  • Artificial intelligence

Thus, investments in defence innovation often produce widespread economic benefits.

Opportunities for Investors and Entrepreneurs

The entry of venture capital funds such as Piper Serica Angel Fund into the defence technology space is another encouraging sign.

Defence startups have traditionally struggled to attract private investment because of long development cycles and regulatory complexity.

However, as government procurement becomes more startup-friendly and the demand for advanced military technology increases, investors are beginning to recognize the sector’s potential.

For entrepreneurs, this creates an unprecedented opportunity.

India’s defence modernization programs require technologies across multiple domains:

  • Robotics and autonomous systems

  • Artificial intelligence

  • Cybersecurity

  • Satellite technologies

  • Advanced sensors

  • Electronic warfare systems

Startups capable of solving these challenges could become the next generation of global defence technology leaders.

The Road Ahead

India stands at a critical moment in the evolution of its defence innovation ecosystem.

The combination of startup participation, regulatory reform, venture capital investment, and strategic government support could transform the country into a global hub for military technology innovation.

The success of companies like Coratia Technologies demonstrates what is possible when startups are given access to defence markets.

Meanwhile, DRDO’s proposal to introduce DARPA-style funding flexibility could unlock a new era of experimentation and technological breakthroughs.

If implemented effectively, these reforms may not only strengthen India’s national security but also position the country as a major exporter of advanced defence technologies.

In the coming decade, India’s defence startups may well become the architects of the next generation of military innovation—both for the nation and the world.
