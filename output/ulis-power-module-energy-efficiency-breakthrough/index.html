ULIS: The Tiny Power Module That Could Transform the World’s Energy Future

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-02-28  | ⏱ 7 min read

 ULIS Power Module: The Tiny Breakthrough That Could Transform Global Energy Efficiency

The world is entering an era of unprecedented electricity demand.

Artificial intelligence data centers, electrification of transport, industrial automation, and expanding digital infrastructure are rapidly increasing global power consumption.

According to global energy analyses, electricity demand is rising at nearly twice the average growth rate seen over the past decade. Massive AI training clusters, cloud infrastructure, and advanced manufacturing plants require enormous amounts of power. Meeting these needs is becoming one of the defining technological and economic challenges of the 21st century.

While expanding renewable energy generation is essential, another equally powerful strategy is to improve the efficiency with which electricity is used and converted. Even small efficiency improvements in power electronics can translate into enormous global energy savings.

A promising innovation addressing this challenge comes from the U.S. Department of Energy’s National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL): a tiny but powerful device called the Ultra-Low Inductance Smart Power Module, or ULIS.

Despite its compact size, ULIS represents a major breakthrough in power electronics design and could significantly improve the efficiency of modern electrical systems.

The Hidden Bottleneck in Electricity Systems

Before understanding the significance of ULIS, it is important to recognize a fundamental challenge in power electronics: power-conversion losses.

Electricity rarely travels directly from generation to use without transformation. Power must constantly be converted between voltages, frequencies, and current levels. These conversions occur in nearly every electrical device:

  • Renewable energy systems

  • Data centers

  • Electric vehicles

  • Aircraft

  • Industrial machines

  • Military platforms

  • Smart grids

The components responsible for this transformation are called power converters, and their performance depends heavily on power semiconductor modules.

However, traditional power modules suffer from a critical limitation called parasitic inductance.

Parasitic inductance is an unwanted electrical resistance created by wiring paths, connectors, and packaging materials inside electronic modules. It slows down current switching and creates energy losses in the form of heat.

In high-power applications, these losses can significantly reduce efficiency and increase cooling requirements.

This is precisely the problem the ULIS module aims to solve.

What Is the ULIS Power Module?


The Ultra-Low Inductance Smart (ULIS) power module is an advanced semiconductor packaging technology designed to dramatically reduce electrical losses during power conversion.

The module uses silicon carbide (SiC) semiconductors, which are already known for superior performance compared with traditional silicon.

Silicon carbide devices can:

  • Handle higher voltages

  • Switch faster

  • Operate at higher temperatures

  • Deliver better efficiency

However, even silicon carbide modules still suffer from packaging limitations that create parasitic inductance.

NREL engineers redesigned the entire module architecture to minimize these internal electrical resistances.

The result is a power module with 7 to 9 times lower parasitic inductance than conventional silicon carbide modules.

This improvement allows electrical current to switch extremely quickly and efficiently.

Five Times the Energy Density

One of the most impressive characteristics of the ULIS design is its extremely high energy density.

Energy density refers to the amount of power a system can deliver relative to its size.

According to NREL researchers, ULIS delivers up to five times the energy density of previous module designs.

This means manufacturers can build systems that are:

  • Smaller

  • Lighter

  • More efficient

  • Easier to integrate

For industries where weight and size matter — such as aerospace and electric mobility — this improvement could be transformative.

The module is rated at 1200 volts and 400 amps, placing it firmly in the category of high-performance industrial power electronics.

Such specifications allow it to support demanding systems, including grid infrastructure, advanced vehicles, and high-performance computing facilities.

A Radical New Architecture

Traditional power modules are often built like compact rectangular bricks containing multiple layers of ceramic substrates and metal interconnects.

ULIS takes a radically different approach.

The module uses a flat octagonal architecture designed to optimize current flow and reduce electrical path lengths. Shorter electrical paths directly reduce parasitic inductance.

In addition, ULIS replaces traditional ceramic materials with flexible polymer-bonded copper layers.

This innovative design offers several benefits:

  • Reduced electrical resistance

  • Improved thermal performance

  • Lower manufacturing cost

  • Increased mechanical reliability

By rethinking the packaging structure rather than just improving semiconductor materials, NREL researchers achieved a dramatic leap in performance.


Intelligent Self-Monitoring Technology

Beyond efficiency improvements, ULIS introduces another critical feature: built-in health monitoring.

The module continuously tracks its internal operating conditions and can detect early warning signs of potential component failure.

This predictive capability is particularly valuable in environments where reliability is mission-critical.

Examples include:

  • Commercial aircraft

  • Electric aviation systems

  • Military vehicles

  • Remote power systems

  • Space applications

For aircraft flying at 30,000 feet or military vehicles operating in extreme conditions, unexpected system failures can have serious consequences.

By identifying potential issues before they occur, ULIS improves safety and operational reliability.

Why Data Centers Need This Technology

One of the fastest-growing consumers of electricity today is the global network of AI and cloud computing data centers.

Training large artificial intelligence models requires enormous computational power, and each server rack consumes significant electricity.

In fact, modern AI facilities may require hundreds of megawatts of power, equivalent to the consumption of small cities.

A substantial portion of that energy is lost in power conversion stages.

Improving conversion efficiency by even a few percentage points could save vast amounts of electricity worldwide.

ULIS modules could help data centers:

  • Reduce energy waste

  • Lower cooling requirements

  • Increase rack power density

  • Improve overall operational efficiency

This is why high-efficiency power electronics are becoming a major focus of research and investment.

Potential Impact on Aviation and Electric Mobility

Electrification is also transforming the transportation sector.

Electric vehicles, hybrid aircraft, and next-generation mobility platforms all depend heavily on advanced power electronics.

For these applications, weight and efficiency are critical.

Higher efficiency translates directly into:

  • Longer range

  • Lower battery requirements

  • Reduced thermal management

  • Better performance

Because ULIS modules are both compact and high-density, they are particularly well-suited for emerging technologies such as:

  • Electric aircraft propulsion

  • Advanced electric vehicles

  • Heavy-duty transport systems

  • Military electric platforms

As transportation continues moving toward electrification, innovations in power electronics will play a crucial role.


A Future-Proof Power Electronics Platform

Another advantage of the ULIS design is its adaptability to future semiconductor materials.

While the current version uses silicon carbide, the architecture could also support emerging materials such as:

  • Gallium Nitride (GaN)

  • Gallium Oxide

  • Advanced wide-bandgap semiconductors

These materials promise even higher switching speeds and greater efficiency.

Because ULIS focuses on improving the packaging and electrical architecture, it can potentially accommodate these next-generation semiconductor technologies.

This makes it a future-ready platform for advanced power conversion systems.

The Bigger Picture: Efficiency as an Energy Resource

When discussing energy solutions, attention often focuses on new power plants or renewable energy generation.

However, improving efficiency across electrical systems can be equally impactful.

Consider this perspective:

If global electrical systems become just 5–10% more efficient, the energy savings could equal the output of hundreds of power plants.

Technologies like ULIS therefore represent an important part of the global energy transition.

They allow us to:

  • Extract more usable power from existing electricity

  • Reduce infrastructure costs

  • Lower carbon emissions

  • Improve the reliability of energy systems

Small innovations in power electronics can ripple across entire energy networks.

Conclusion

The ULIS power module may be small in size, but its implications are enormous.

By dramatically reducing parasitic inductance, increasing energy density, and integrating intelligent monitoring, this technology could redefine how electricity is converted and managed in modern systems.

From AI data centers and renewable grids to electric aircraft and advanced military platforms, efficient power conversion is becoming increasingly important.

As global electricity demand continues rising, innovations like ULIS demonstrate that solving the energy challenge is not only about producing more power — it is also about using every watt more intelligently.

If widely adopted, this tiny semiconductor module could play a surprisingly large role in shaping the future of global energy systems.
