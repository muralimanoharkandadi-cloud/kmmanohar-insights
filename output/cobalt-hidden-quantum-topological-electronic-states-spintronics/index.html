Cobalt’s Hidden Quantum Power: A Familiar Metal Reveals Extraordinary Quantum Behavior

For more than four decades, cobalt has been considered one of the most thoroughly understood ferromagnetic metals.

Its atomic structure, magnetic behavior, and electronic properties have served as foundational examples in textbooks and research laboratories.

However, a groundbreaking study led by physicist Dr. Jaime Sánchez-Barriga from Helmholtz-Zentrum Berlin (HZB) has revealed that this familiar element hides an unexpected layer of quantum complexity.

Using advanced synchrotron experiments at BESSY II, scientists have discovered that cobalt hosts topological electronic structures known as magnetic nodal lines—a rare quantum phenomenon that could enable entirely new technologies in electronics and information processing.

This discovery challenges long-standing assumptions about ferromagnetic metals and opens a new frontier in quantum materials research.

Looking Inside Cobalt’s Electronic Structure

To uncover these hidden properties, researchers used a highly sophisticated technique called spin- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (spin-ARPES).

This experimental method allows scientists to observe three key characteristics of electrons inside a material:

  • Their energy

  • Their momentum

  • Their spin orientation

By mapping these properties with exceptional precision, the team identified intertwined electronic bands inside cobalt’s crystal lattice.

Instead of remaining separate, these bands intersect along extended paths inside the material’s momentum space, forming magnetic nodal lines.

These crossings are not isolated points but continuous pathways where two spin-polarized electronic states meet without opening an energy gap.

What Are Magnetic Nodal Lines?

Magnetic nodal lines are special quantum features where electronic energy bands cross each other in a stable, protected manner.

They possess several remarkable properties:

1. Gapless electronic states
Electrons can move freely at these crossing points, leading to extremely efficient charge transport.

2. Topological protection

The crossings are protected by the material’s crystal symmetry, meaning they remain stable even when disturbances occur.

3. Spin polarization

Because cobalt is ferromagnetic, the electrons along these nodal lines carry a net spin orientation.

This makes them highly attractive for spintronics, a field of technology that uses electron spin instead of charge to process information.

Magnetic Control of Quantum States

One of the most fascinating aspects of this discovery is that cobalt’s quantum states can be controlled magnetically.

Ferromagnetism breaks time-reversal symmetry, meaning the electronic states behave differently depending on the direction of magnetization.

Researchers found that by reversing the magnetization direction, they could:

  • Flip the spin polarization of the nodal lines

  • Control the spin texture of electrons

  • Potentially open or close electronic gaps

This ability acts like a magnetic switch, which could be crucial for designing future quantum and spin-based electronic devices.

Massless Electrons Inside a Metal

Another surprising observation is how electrons behave near the nodal line crossings.

Near these intersections, electrons behave like massless relativistic particles, similar to photons or particles found in exotic materials like graphene.

This behavior allows electrons to travel extremely fast through the material, enabling:

  • High-speed electronic transport

  • Reduced energy loss

  • Efficient signal processing

  • Such properties are highly desirable for next-generation computing and communication technologies.

Theory Confirms the Discovery

To validate the experimental results, the research team conducted first-principles calculations using density functional theory (DFT).

These theoretical models revealed:

  • The exact locations of nodal lines within cobalt’s electronic structure

  • Their protection by crystalline mirror symmetries

  • Their persistence even in the presence of spin-orbit coupling

The theoretical predictions showed excellent agreement with the experimental observations, strengthening confidence in the discovery.

Why This Discovery Matters

This finding is significant for several reasons.

1. A New Quantum Platform

Cobalt could become a model material for studying the interaction between magnetism and topology.

2. Spintronic Applications

The ability to control spin-polarized electrons with magnetic fields could lead to more efficient:

  • Magnetic memory

  • Quantum sensors

  • Spin-based processors

3. Room-Temperature Stability

Unlike many quantum materials that require extremely low temperatures, cobalt’s topological features remain stable at room temperature, making them far more practical for real-world technologies.

4. Hidden Physics in Familiar Materials Perhaps the most surprising lesson is that even well-known elements can still hide undiscovered quantum phenomena.

A New Frontier in Ferromagnetic Materials

The discovery suggests that similar topological electronic structures might exist in other elemental and transition-metal ferromagnets.

Future research may explore:

  • Interfaces between cobalt and heavy elements

  • Two-dimensional versions of magnetic metals

  • Engineering nodal-line states in thin films

These investigations could reveal entirely new classes of quantum materials.

The Bigger Scientific Lesson

Cobalt has been studied for decades and is widely used in industries ranging from batteries to aerospace alloys.

Yet this research shows that even such familiar materials may still hold hidden quantum landscapes waiting to be discovered.

As scientists continue probing deeper into the electronic structures of matter, discoveries like this remind us that the quantum world often hides extraordinary surprises in the most ordinary places.
