Mars, Venus & a Space Station by 2035 : India’s Next Space Leap:

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-01  | ⏱ 8 min read

 India’s Next Space Frontier: From Mangalyaan-2 to.... 

Bharatiya Antariksha Station

Introduction

Over the last decade, India has quietly transformed itself into one of the most credible and cost-efficient spacefaring nations in the world. From launching interplanetary missions at record-low costs to achieving complex lunar landings and building indigenous navigation systems, the Indian space program has demonstrated that innovation, discipline, and scientific vision can compensate for limited resources.

Now the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) is preparing for a far more ambitious phase. The coming decade will witness missions to Mars, Venus, and the Moon, the development of human spaceflight capabilities, the establishment of India’s own space station, and a dramatic increase in commercial launch activities.

Several major projects currently under development include:

  • Mars Orbiter Mission-2 (Mangalyaan-2)

  • Venus Orbiter Mission (Shukrayaan)

  • Lunar Polar Exploration Mission

  • Bharatiya Antariksha Station

  • Expanded commercial launch capability targeting 50 launches in five years

Together, these missions mark the transition of India from a cost-efficient launch provider to a comprehensive space power capable of planetary exploration, human spaceflight, and orbital infrastructure.

This article explores these missions in detail, examining their objectives, technological significance, and the strategic impact they will have on India’s scientific and economic future.

The Legacy of India’s First Mars Mission

Before discussing the upcoming Mars mission, it is important to understand the significance of India’s first interplanetary success.

In 2013, India launched the Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), popularly known as Mangalyaan. When the spacecraft successfully entered Mars orbit in 2014, India achieved something unprecedented:

  • It became the first nation to reach Mars orbit on its first attempt

  • It became the fourth space agency in the world to reach Mars

  • The mission was completed at a cost of roughly $74 million

The mission studied the Martian atmosphere, surface features, and methane presence while demonstrating India’s ability to execute complex interplanetary navigation.

More importantly, Mangalyaan proved that deep-space exploration could be achieved with frugal engineering and intelligent mission planning.

Building on the success of the original mission, ISRO has begun developing Mars Orbiter Mission-2, widely known as Mangalyaan-2.

Expected Timeline

The mission is expected to launch around 2026, although final timelines may depend on launch windows and technological readiness.

Mission Objectives

Unlike the first mission, which focused mainly on orbital observations, Mangalyaan-2 is expected to introduce advanced exploration capabilities, potentially including:

  • Development of Mars landing technologies

  • Improved atmospheric and geological studies

  • High-resolution surface imaging

  • Study of Martian dust storms and climate

Technological Significance

The inclusion of landing technology development is particularly important. Successfully landing on Mars remains one of the most difficult challenges in space exploration due to:

  • Thin Martian atmosphere

  • Complex entry-descent-landing (EDL) sequences

  • Communication delays with Earth

If India successfully demonstrates such technologies, it would place the country among a small group capable of surface exploration on Mars.

Strategic Importance

The mission will also:

  • Strengthen India’s planetary science research

  • Enable international collaboration in Mars studies

  • Contribute data for future human exploration missions

Mars remains one of the most studied planets because of its potential for past microbial life and future human habitation.

While Mars has traditionally attracted more attention, scientists increasingly recognize the importance of studying Venus, often referred to as Earth’s twin planet.

India’s Venus Orbiter Mission, known as Shukrayaan, is expected to launch around 2028–2029.

Why Venus Matters

Venus provides a unique opportunity to understand planetary evolution. Despite being similar to Earth in size and composition, Venus evolved into an extremely hostile world with:

  • Surface temperatures exceeding 450°C

  • Atmospheric pressure is 90 times that of Earth

  • Thick clouds of sulfuric acid

Understanding why Venus developed such conditions could provide crucial insights into climate evolution and planetary habitability.

Mission Goals

Shukrayaan will focus on several scientific objectives:

  • Studying Venus’ atmosphere and cloud layers

  • Mapping surface features and volcanic activity

  • Understanding solar wind interactions

  • Investigating atmospheric chemistry and greenhouse effects

Scientific Instruments

The spacecraft will likely carry instruments such as:

  • Radar imaging systems

  • Spectrometers for atmospheric analysis

  • Plasma analyzers

  • Thermal imaging sensors

These tools will allow scientists to better understand the planet’s dynamic climate system and geological history.

Global Context

Interest in Venus exploration is rising globally. NASA, ESA, and other agencies have announced future Venus missions. India’s participation ensures it remains part of the next major wave of planetary exploration.

Lunar Polar Exploration: The Next Step After Chandrayaan

India’s lunar exploration journey has seen remarkable milestones, especially with the success of Chandrayaan-3, which achieved a historic soft landing near the Moon’s south pole.

Building on this success, India is preparing a Lunar Polar Exploration Mission planned for the late 2020s.

Focus on the Lunar South Pole

The Moon’s south pole is one of the most scientifically interesting locations in the solar system because:

  • It contains permanently shadowed craters

  • These regions may hold significant water-ice deposits

  • Water can support future human settlements and fuel production

Mission Objectives

The mission aims to:

  • Study lunar water resources

  • Conduct surface mobility experiments

  • Analyze lunar soil composition

  • Test technologies for long-term lunar habitation

International Collaboration

This mission may involve international partnerships, reflecting the growing trend of collaborative space exploration.

Bharatiya Antariksha Station: India’s Future Space Habitat

Perhaps the most ambitious component of India’s space roadmap is the development of Bharatiya Antariksha Station, the country’s own space station planned around 2035.

Why a Space Station?

Space stations serve as orbital laboratories where astronauts can conduct research in microgravity conditions. Such environments enable experiments that are impossible on Earth.

Key research areas include:

  • Material science

  • Biotechnology

  • Fluid physics

  • Medical research

Structure and Capabilities

India’s proposed space station is expected to be a modular facility assembled in orbit over multiple launches.

The station will support:

  • Long-duration astronaut missions

  • Scientific research

  • Technology demonstrations

  • Training for future deep-space missions

Link to Human Spaceflight

The space station will be built on India’s Gaganyaan human spaceflight program, which aims to send Indian astronauts to low-Earth orbit.

Once operational, Bharatiya Antariksha Station could become the foundation for future lunar and Mars missions involving human crews.

Expanding Launch Capacity: 50 Launches in Five Years

India’s ambitions are not limited to scientific exploration. The country is also aiming to expand its presence in the global commercial space market.

ISRO plans to conduct around 50 rocket launches in the next five years, significantly increasing launch frequency.

Key Launch Vehicles

India currently operates several launch systems:

PSLV (Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle)
Known for reliability and versatility.

GSLV (Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle)
Used for heavier payloads.

LVM-3 (Launch Vehicle Mark-3)
India’s heavy-lift rocket is the vehicle selected for human spaceflight missions.

Role of the Private Sector

India has opened the space sector to private companies through policy reforms and institutions such as IN-SPACe.

This has encouraged startups working on:

  • Small satellite launch vehicles

  • Space robotics

  • Satellite manufacturing

  • Space data services

These developments are expected to transform India into a major player in the global space economy.

Economic and Strategic Impact

India’s expanding space program will generate benefits across multiple sectors.

Technological Advancement

Space missions drive innovation in areas such as:

  • Advanced materials

  • Artificial intelligence

  • Communications technology

  • Robotics and automation

Industrial Growth

A stronger space ecosystem encourages the growth of:

  • Aerospace manufacturing

  • Electronics and semiconductor industries

  • Satellite services

National Security

Space capabilities also strengthen:

  • Communication infrastructure

  • Navigation systems

  • Earth observation and disaster monitoring

Inspiration for Future Generations

Perhaps the most important impact is the inspiration provided to millions of students and young scientists across India.

Space exploration stimulates curiosity, scientific thinking, and technological ambition.

India’s Vision for the Next Decade

The coming decade represents a transformational period for India’s space program.

The country’s roadmap includes:

  • Planetary exploration missions

  • Human spaceflight

  • Orbital infrastructure

  • Commercial launch expansion

With strong government support, an emerging private sector, and growing international collaboration, India is positioning itself as one of the leading space powers of the 21st century.

Conclusion

India’s space journey began modestly in the 1960s with sounding rockets launched from a small coastal facility. Today, the country stands on the threshold of a new era of exploration and technological capability.

From Mangalyaan-2’s ambitious Mars mission to Shukrayaan’s exploration of Venus, from lunar polar studies to the creation of Bharatiya Antariksha Station, the vision for the future is bold and far-reaching.

Combined with a dramatic increase in launch frequency and growing commercial participation, these developments indicate that India is not merely participating in the global space race—it is helping shape the future of humanity’s exploration of space.

The next decade may well witness India transforming from a cost-efficient space nation into a global leader in space science, exploration, and orbital infrastructure.

And the journey is only just beginning.
