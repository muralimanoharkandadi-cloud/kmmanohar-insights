India’s Strategic Self-Reliance: Critical Minerals and Semiconductors

India’s vision of Atmanirbhar Bharat (Self-Reliant India) is evolving into a focused strategy aimed at strengthening sectors where foreign dependency could become a strategic vulnerability.

Two sectors stand out prominently in this effort:

Critical Minerals and Semiconductors.

Both are essential to the future of technology, energy, defence, and digital infrastructure.

India’s recalibrated approach reflects a deeper understanding that economic strength today is closely tied to control over foundational resources and technologies. As global supply chains become increasingly fragmented due to geopolitical tensions and trade realignments, countries are prioritizing resilience over cost efficiency. In this context, India’s focused push toward critical sectors is not only timely but essential for ensuring long-term stability and competitiveness in a rapidly evolving global order.

Why Critical Minerals Matter

Critical minerals such as lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare earth elements are vital for modern technologies.

They power:

  • Electric vehicles

  • Renewable energy storage

  • Electronics and smartphones

  • Defence technologies

  • Advanced manufacturing

India currently imports almost all of these minerals, making the economy vulnerable to supply disruptions.

China dominates global rare-earth processing and battery mineral supply chains, while other resources are concentrated in a few countries such as Australia, Chile, and the Democratic Republic of Congo.

Recognizing this risk, India has launched initiatives to:

  • Identify domestic reserves

  • Secure overseas mining assets

  • Build refining and processing capabilities

The goal is not complete independence but strategic resilience.

Beyond immediate industrial applications, critical minerals are also central to India’s climate commitments and clean energy targets. The transition to renewable energy systems, including solar and wind, depends heavily on efficient storage solutions powered by these minerals. Without secure access, India’s ambitions in electric mobility and green hydrogen could face significant delays, making supply chain security a key enabler of both economic and environmental goals.

The Semiconductor Imperative

Semiconductors are the brain of modern technology.

They power everything from smartphones and automobiles to satellites and defence systems.

Despite being one of the world’s fastest-growing digital economies, India has historically depended entirely on imported chips.

This creates risks in sectors such as:

  • Consumer electronics

  • Automobile manufacturing

  • Defence equipment

  • Telecommunications infrastructure

To address this, the Government of India launched the Semiconductor Mission, backed by a ₹76,000-crore incentive package.

This initiative aims to attract global manufacturers and develop an ecosystem that includes:

  • Chip fabrication plants (fabs)

  • Semiconductor packaging units

  • Research and design centers

  • Skilled workforce development

Companies such as Micron and several international semiconductor firms have already shown interest in building manufacturing facilities in India.

Additionally, the semiconductor ecosystem extends far beyond fabrication units. It includes design, testing, packaging, and supply of specialty chemicals and equipment. India already has a strong base in chip design talent, with several global companies operating R&D centers in the country. Leveraging this strength while building manufacturing capabilities can create a holistic ecosystem, generating employment, boosting exports, and enhancing technological sovereignty.

Building Strategic Supply Chains

India’s approach to self-reliance focuses on diversification rather than isolation.

The strategy includes:

  • Partnerships with resource-rich countries

  • Participation in Indo-Pacific supply chain frameworks

  • Development of domestic manufacturing capacity

  • Strengthening research and innovation

By combining domestic capability with global collaboration, India aims to reduce strategic vulnerabilities while integrating deeper into global technology networks.

A key aspect of this strategy is reducing concentration risk by avoiding overdependence on a single geography or supplier. By spreading sourcing and manufacturing across multiple trusted partners, India can cushion itself against disruptions such as trade restrictions, conflicts, or pandemics. This multi-aligned approach also strengthens India’s position as a reliable partner in global supply chains, opening up new avenues for trade and investment.

The Road Ahead

The global economy is entering an era where technology supply chains determine economic power.

Critical minerals fuel the clean-energy transition, while semiconductors power the digital economy.

India’s push toward self-reliance in these sectors is therefore not just about manufacturing.

It is about economic resilience, technological leadership, and national security.

If executed effectively, this strategy could position India as a key player in the emerging global technology landscape.

However, sustained progress will depend on consistent policy support, infrastructure development, and ease of doing business. Encouraging private sector participation, fostering innovation, and ensuring regulatory stability will be critical in maintaining momentum. With the right execution, India has the potential not only to meet its domestic demand but also to emerge as a global hub for next-generation technologies.

Conclusion 

Ultimately, the journey toward self-reliance is a gradual process that requires alignment between policy, industry, and global partnerships. While challenges remain, India’s strategic clarity and targeted initiatives indicate a strong foundation for future growth. The coming decade will be decisive in determining how effectively the country can convert its ambitions into tangible outcomes.
