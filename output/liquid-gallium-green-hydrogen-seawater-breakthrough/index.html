Liquid Gallium Breakthrough Could Enable Circular Green Hydrogen Production From Seawater

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-02-28  | ⏱ 5 min read

Liquid Gallium Breakthrough: Scientists Discover a Circular Method to Produce Green Hydrogen From Seawater

How can gallium produce hydrogen from water?

Scientists discovered that when liquid gallium particles suspended in water are exposed to light, they undergo a surface oxidation reaction that releases hydrogen gas while forming gallium oxyhydroxide. The byproduct can then be converted back into gallium, creating a circular and reusable hydrogen production process.

Gallium is a fascinating metal with unusual properties.

Although it looks solid at room temperature, it melts at around 30°C (86°F)—close to human body temperature—forming shiny pools of liquid metal.

Normally, gallium has a chemically non-reactive surface, meaning most materials do not stick or react with it easily. However, researchers discovered that when gallium is placed in water and exposed to sunlight or artificial light, the surface chemistry changes dramatically.

Under illumination, gallium undergoes slow oxidation, which triggers a reaction with water molecules.

This process releases hydrogen gas while forming gallium oxyhydroxide as a byproduct.

The key innovation lies in the recyclability of the metal. Scientists can convert gallium oxyhydroxide back into pure gallium, allowing the same material to be reused repeatedly.

This creates what researchers describe as a circular hydrogen production system.

A simple liquid metal reaction powered by sunlight could transform the future of clean hydrogen energy.

The global race to develop efficient and affordable green hydrogen has taken a surprising turn. Scientists have discovered that liquid gallium, a shiny metal that melts near body temperature, can generate hydrogen gas simply by reacting with water under light.

Researchers from the University of Sydney have demonstrated a new circular hydrogen production process where gallium particles suspended in water react with light to produce hydrogen while forming a recyclable compound called gallium oxyhydroxide.

Even more promising: the system works with both freshwater and seawater, eliminating one of the biggest cost barriers in traditional hydrogen production technologies.

This discovery, published in the journal Nature Communications, could open a new pathway toward scalable, sustainable hydrogen fuel production.

The experimental system developed by the research team is surprisingly simple.

Tiny gallium particles are dispersed in water, either freshwater or seawater.

When exposed to sunlight or artificial light, the following steps occur:

  1. Gallium absorbs light energy.

  2. The metal surface oxidizes in water.

  3. Hydrogen gas is released.

  4. Gallium oxyhydroxide forms as a byproduct.

  5. The compound can be chemically reduced back to gallium.

This process allows continuous hydrogen generation without consuming the metal permanently.

Why Hydrogen Is So Important for the Future

Hydrogen is widely considered one of the most promising clean energy carriers.

When hydrogen burns or is used in fuel cells, the only byproduct is water, making it a powerful tool for reducing carbon emissions.

Key industries looking toward hydrogen include:

  • Steel manufacturing

  • Aviation fuels

  • Shipping and heavy transport

  • Grid-scale energy storage

  • Chemical manufacturing

However, most hydrogen today is produced from natural gas, a process that emits large amounts of carbon dioxide.

Green hydrogen aims to change this by using renewable energy to split water into hydrogen and oxygen.

The Challenge With Current Hydrogen Technologies

The most common green hydrogen production method today is electrolysis, which splits water using electricity.

But electrolysis faces several limitations:

• Requires large amounts of electricity
• Often needs high-purity water
• Infrastructure costs remain high
• Energy efficiency is still improving

Other experimental techniques, such as photocatalysis and plasma-based splitting, are still in early development stages.

The gallium-based system could overcome several of these barriers.

Most importantly, it can operate with seawater, which is abundant and inexpensive.

Advantages of the Gallium Hydrogen Process

Researchers believe the technology offers several key advantages:

✔ Works with seawater or freshwater
✔ Driven by light instead of electricity alone
✔ Reusable metal catalyst in a circular cycle
✔ Operates at low temperatures
✔ Potentially scalable for industrial reactors

According to the research team, the next step is to build a mid-scale reactor to evaluate commercial viability.

If successful, this technology could help regions with strong solar resources develop large-scale hydrogen production facilities.

Challenges That Still Need to Be Solved

Despite its promise, several questions remain before commercialization.

Experts point to several engineering challenges:

• Energy cost of regenerating gallium from gallium oxyhydroxide
• Hydrogen storage and transportation infrastructure
• Materials compatibility due to hydrogen embrittlement
• Economic feasibility compared with electrolysis

As with many scientific breakthroughs, translating laboratory success into industrial reality will require years of development.

A New Chapter in the Hydrogen Economy

The discovery highlights an important truth about innovation: sometimes simple chemistry can unlock transformative energy solutions.

Liquid gallium reacting with water may seem like a small scientific curiosity. But if the technology scales effectively, it could help build a circular, solar-driven hydrogen production system.

For countries with abundant sunlight and access to seawater, such as Australia, India, and Middle Eastern nations, this approach could provide a competitive advantage in the emerging global hydrogen economy.

The coming decade will determine whether this shimmering liquid metal becomes a cornerstone of the world’s clean energy future.
