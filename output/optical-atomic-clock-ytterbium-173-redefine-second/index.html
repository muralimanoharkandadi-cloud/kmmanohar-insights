A New Optical Atomic Clock Using Ytterbium-173 Could Redefine the Second

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-17  | ⏱ 3 min read

Introduction -

Time is the invisible backbone of modern civilization—from GPS navigation to financial systems and global communications. 


For decades, the world has relied on cesium atomic clocks as the gold standard for defining the second.

But a quiet revolution is underway. Scientists are now developing optical atomic clocks that are so precise they could redefine time itself. At the forefront of this shift is a new breakthrough involving ytterbium-173 ions, which may shape the future of time measurement.

4) What Defines a Second Today?

The current definition of a second is based on cesium-133 atoms, which oscillate at microwave frequencies. These oscillations act like a pendulum—except far more precise.

However, as technology advances, even this precision is no longer sufficient for emerging applications like quantum networks and deep-space navigation.

Optical clocks use laser light instead of microwaves, operating at frequencies nearly 100,000 times higher.

This allows:

  • Finer time divisions

  • Greater measurement precision

  • Reduced uncertainty

In simple terms, optical clocks can “tick” much faster—and more accurately.

6) The Ytterbium-173 Breakthrough

A collaborative effort between Germany’s PTB and Thailand’s NIMT has introduced a promising innovation: a multi-ion optical clock using ytterbium-173.

Key highlights:

  • Uses multiple ions for improved stability

  • Maintains the precision of single-ion systems

  • Exploits the unique nuclear properties of ytterbium-173

One standout feature is its long-lived excited state, allowing more stable and reliable measurements.

7) Accuracy vs Stability: The Core Challenge

Traditionally:

  • Single-ion clocks → extremely accurate but less stable

  • Multi-particle clocks → stable but slightly less precise

The ytterbium-173 system aims to merge both advantages, solving one of the biggest challenges in timekeeping science.

8) Quantum Computing Implications

This breakthrough goes beyond timekeeping.

Because ytterbium-173 allows precise control of multiple quantum states, it can:

  • Act as a multi-qubit platform

  • Enable more efficient quantum information storage

  • Improve quantum gate operations

This positions it as a strong candidate for next-generation quantum technologies.

9) Impact on Fundamental Physics

The research also opens doors to deeper scientific exploration:

  • Measurement of atomic state lifetimes

  • Insights into nuclear structure

  • Testing theories beyond the Standard Model of physics

Such precision could help detect subtle physical effects previously impossible to observe.

10) The Future of Timekeeping

The global scientific community is now evaluating multiple optical clock designs. While no single system has been chosen yet, ytterbium-173 clocks are emerging as a strong contender.

Within the next decade, we may witness:

  • A redefinition of the second

  • Global adoption of optical clocks

  • Ultra-precise synchronization across technologies

11) Conclusion

The development of ytterbium-173 optical atomic clocks marks a significant step toward redefining time itself. By combining accuracy, stability, and quantum control, this innovation could influence everything from computing to cosmology.

Time, as we know it, may soon be measured in an entirely new way—guided by the precision of light and quantum physics.
